Wildlife in the Dominican Republic: What You Might See and Where to Look

By Dre · May 22, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

Most visitors come to the DR for the beaches and leave without realising what else was around them the whole time. After 30+ years here, I've learned to look — and I want to show you what to look for.

The Dominican Republic is not a country people typically associate with wildlife watching. They come for the sea, the sun, the rum, and the music — and those things are all here. But the island of Hispaniola is one of the most biologically distinct places in the Caribbean, home to species found nowhere else on earth, and if you know where to look, the wildlife encounters can be genuinely extraordinary.

I've been watching the animals here for more than thirty years. Some of what I've seen has stayed with me in a way that no beach ever has. Here is a guide to what you might encounter, and how to find it.

The Rhinoceros Iguana

The rhinoceros iguana is the most striking animal you're likely to see in the DR, and one of the most impressive reptiles in the Caribbean. They can reach over a metre in length, with a heavy, armoured body and the distinctive horn-like projections on their snout that give them their name. They look prehistoric — because they essentially are.

Around Juan Dolio, you'll see smaller iguanas regularly in rocky areas near the coast. The larger rhinoceros iguanas are more common in the drier, more remote parts of the country — the southwest, around Lago Enriquillo, is the best place to see them in numbers. But even the common green iguanas you'll spot sunning themselves on walls and rocks near the guest house are worth a moment's attention. They've been here far longer than we have.

💡 Dre's Tip: Don't try to feed or touch the iguanas. They're wild animals and they bite — hard. Watch from a respectful distance and they'll go about their business without concern. That's the best kind of wildlife encounter: one where the animal doesn't notice you.

Birds: A Birder's Destination

Hispaniola is one of the most important birding destinations in the Caribbean. The island has over 300 recorded bird species, including around 30 that are endemic — found nowhere else in the world. The Hispaniolan parrot, the Hispaniolan trogon, the palmchat (the national bird, a sociable species that builds enormous communal nests in royal palms), and the Hispaniolan woodpecker are all species you can see without travelling far from Juan Dolio.

The best birding is in the early morning, before the heat builds. The Los Haitises National Park, about two hours from Juan Dolio, is one of the finest birding sites in the Caribbean — a landscape of limestone karst hills, mangroves, and tropical forest that supports an extraordinary range of species. I take guests there regularly, and it never disappoints.

"The first time I heard a Hispaniolan trogon call in the forest, I stopped walking and just listened. It's one of those sounds that makes you understand why people become birders." — Dre

Humpback Whales: A January to March Spectacle

Every year, between January and March, humpback whales gather in Samaná Bay on the northeast coast of the DR to breed. It is one of the largest humpback whale gatherings in the North Atlantic — thousands of whales in a relatively small area, breaching, singing, and competing for mates. If you're visiting during those months, a whale-watching trip from Las Terrenas or Samaná town is not optional. It is one of the great wildlife spectacles of the Caribbean.

The rest of the year, the bay is quieter, but the journey to Samaná is worth making regardless — the peninsula is one of the most beautiful parts of the DR, with a different character from the south coast where Juan Dolio sits.

Sea Turtles

Three species of sea turtle nest on Dominican beaches: the leatherback, the loggerhead, and the hawksbill. Leatherbacks are the largest — they can weigh over 500 kilograms — and they nest on beaches along the north and east coasts between March and July. The nesting season is a remarkable thing to witness, but it requires patience, a guide who knows the beaches, and a willingness to sit quietly in the dark for a long time.

Around Juan Dolio, turtle sightings in the water are occasional but not rare. If you're snorkelling on the reef and you see a large dark shape moving slowly below you, stay calm and still — it may well be a hawksbill going about its business. They're not afraid of people, but they don't like to be chased.

💡 Dre's Tip: If you want to see nesting turtles, contact a local conservation organisation rather than going independently. The beaches where turtles nest are often protected, and having a knowledgeable guide ensures you see the turtles without disturbing them. It also means your visit supports the conservation work directly.

The Solenodon: The DR's Most Unusual Resident

The Hispaniolan solenodon is one of the strangest mammals alive. It's a small, shrew-like creature that has survived essentially unchanged since the age of the dinosaurs — one of the very few venomous mammals in the world, injecting venom through grooved teeth when it bites. It's nocturnal, secretive, and increasingly rare, threatened by habitat loss and introduced predators.

You are unlikely to see one. I have seen one, once, in thirty years — a brief encounter on a forest trail at night that I still think about. But knowing it's out there, in the forests of this island, is part of what makes the DR remarkable. The solenodon is a reminder that this place has a deep natural history that most visitors never encounter.

How to Watch Responsibly

The wildlife of the DR has survived centuries of human pressure — hunting, habitat destruction, introduced species. What remains is worth protecting. The principles are simple: don't feed wild animals, don't touch them, don't disturb nesting sites, and don't buy products made from protected species. If you're taking a tour, choose operators who follow these principles. The best wildlife encounters are the ones where the animal is undisturbed and you are simply a quiet observer.

A Day Tour with Dre: What to Expect When You Explore the DR with a Local

By Dre · June 16, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've been guiding visitors around the Dominican Republic for more than thirty years. Every tour is different because every guest is different. Here's what the experience actually looks like.

Most people who visit the Dominican Republic see the same things: the resort pool, the beach in front of the hotel, maybe a bus trip to Altos de Chavón or a catamaran to Saona Island. These are fine. They are also what every other tourist sees, in the same order, with the same commentary.

A day tour with me is something different. It starts with a conversation — what do you actually want to see? What matters to you? History, nature, food, local life, the coast, the mountains? The answer shapes everything that follows. No two tours I run are identical, and that's deliberate.

How the Day Begins

We start early. The Dominican Republic is at its best in the morning — the light is softer, the roads are quieter, and the heat hasn't built yet. I pick guests up from Guest House Holland at seven or half past, and we're moving before the day gets complicated.

The first stop is usually a local breakfast. Not a hotel buffet — a comedor or a roadside spot where the coffee is strong, the mangú is freshly made, and the bill for two people comes to less than three dollars. This is where the day starts to feel different from a package tour. You're eating what the people who live here eat, in the places they actually go.

"The best part of any tour isn't the destination — it's the twenty minutes you spend at a roadside comedor watching the morning happen. That's the Dominican Republic that most visitors never see." — Dre

What We See Along the Way

The route depends on the guest, but there are places I return to again and again because they consistently surprise people. The coastline east of Juan Dolio, away from the resort strip, where the beach is wild and the water is clear and there is nobody selling you anything. The sugar cane fields inland, where the landscape shifts completely and you understand something about the history of this island that no museum can quite convey.

I know the reef well enough to tell you exactly where to enter the water and what you'll find there. I know which villages have markets on which days, and which colmados stock the cold Presidente that tastes better than any beer you've had all week. I know the roads that don't appear on maps and the viewpoints that don't appear in guidebooks.

💡 Dre's Tip: Bring a hat, sunscreen, and comfortable shoes. Bring less than you think you need and more water than you think you need. Leave the itinerary anxiety at the guest house — the best moments on any tour are the unplanned ones, and those only happen when you're not watching the clock.

Lunch: The Real Dominican Kitchen

Lunch is always local. I have places I trust — family-run restaurants where the menu is whatever was fresh that morning, where the portions are generous and the prices are honest. Pescado frito with tostones and rice, or a slow-cooked sancocho if we're somewhere inland. The kind of food that takes time to make and tastes like it.

This is often the part of the day guests remember most. Not a famous landmark or a scenic viewpoint, but a meal at a plastic table under a palm roof, with a cold drink and no particular reason to hurry.

The Afternoon and the Return

The afternoon is flexible. Sometimes we continue to a second destination — a waterfall, a colonial town, a stretch of coast I want to show someone. Sometimes we head back slowly, stopping wherever something catches the eye. I've been doing this long enough to know that the best tours don't follow a rigid schedule. They follow the day.

We're usually back at Guest House Holland by late afternoon, in time for a swim and a rest before the evening. Guests leave with photographs, full stomachs, and a version of the Dominican Republic that most visitors never encounter.

That's the point. Thirty years of living here is worth something. I'd rather share it than keep it to myself.

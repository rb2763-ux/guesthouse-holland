What to Pack for the Dominican Republic: Dre's Honest List

By Dre · May 19, 2026 · 5 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've watched guests arrive with enormous suitcases full of things they never use, and others arrive with almost nothing and spend their first day shopping. After 30+ years in the DR, I know what you actually need.

Packing for the Dominican Republic is simpler than most people make it. The climate is consistent — warm, humid, occasionally rainy — and the country has everything you need if you forget something. But there are a few things that are genuinely hard to find here, or expensive when you do, and a few things that most packing lists include that you simply don't need. Here is my honest version.

Clothing: Less Than You Think

The DR is hot. Not "warm" hot — genuinely, persistently hot, with humidity that makes everything feel warmer than the thermometer says. Light, breathable fabrics are not optional; they're the difference between enjoying your day and spending it uncomfortable. Cotton and linen work well. Synthetics that don't breathe will make you miserable by midday.

For Juan Dolio specifically: you need beachwear, a couple of light shirts or dresses for evenings, one pair of trousers or a skirt if you're planning to visit restaurants or the Colonial Zone in Santo Domingo, and comfortable walking shoes. That's essentially it. The guest house has laundry facilities, so you don't need to pack for every day of your trip.

💡 Dre's Tip: Pack one outfit you'd be comfortable wearing to a slightly nicer restaurant. Not formal — the DR is not a formal country — but something that isn't beach clothes. You'll want it for an evening in Santo Domingo or a dinner at one of the better places in Juan Dolio, and you'll feel better for having it.

Sun Protection: Don't Underestimate the Caribbean Sun

The sun here is not the same sun you know from Europe or North America. The DR sits close to the equator, and the UV index on a clear day is genuinely extreme. People who spend their first day on the beach without adequate protection end up spending their second day indoors, and that's a waste of a trip.

Bring high-factor sunscreen — SPF 50 at minimum — and bring more of it than you think you need. Sunscreen is available in the DR but it's expensive and the selection is limited. A good hat with a wide brim is not optional if you're planning to spend time outdoors. Sunglasses with UV protection are worth the investment if you don't already have them.

"I've seen people arrive from the Netherlands in May thinking they know what sun feels like. They do not. The Caribbean sun is a different thing entirely. Respect it." — Dre

Medications and Health Essentials

Bring any prescription medications you take, with enough supply for your entire trip plus a few extra days. Pharmacies in the DR are generally well-stocked, but matching a specific prescription from another country can be complicated and time-consuming.

A basic travel health kit is worth putting together before you leave: ibuprofen or paracetamol, something for stomach upsets (traveller's diarrhoea is not common here but it happens, usually from eating at places with lower hygiene standards), antihistamines if you're prone to insect reactions, and a good insect repellent. Mosquitoes are present in the DR, particularly in the evenings and near standing water. A DEET-based repellent is the most effective option.

💡 Dre's Tip: Bring a small tube of hydrocortisone cream. Insect bites in the tropics can be more irritating than you're used to, and having something to reduce the inflammation makes a real difference. It's cheap and takes up almost no space.

Electronics and Practical Items

The DR uses the same electrical outlets and voltage as the United States (110V, Type A/B plugs). If you're coming from Europe, you'll need a travel adapter — and a voltage converter if your devices don't support dual voltage, though most modern electronics do. Check the label on your charger before you travel.

A portable power bank is worth having. Power cuts are not frequent in Juan Dolio, but they do happen, and having a charged backup for your phone is useful. A waterproof phone case or dry bag is worth considering if you're planning water activities — the sea and your phone are not natural companions.

What to Leave Behind

Formal clothing: you won't need it. The DR is casual, and even the better restaurants in Santo Domingo don't require anything approaching formal dress. Heavy books: the guest house has a small library, and your phone or e-reader will serve you better in the heat. Excessive toiletries: the basics are available everywhere, and the weight isn't worth it. Hair dryers and straighteners: the humidity will undo whatever you do to your hair within twenty minutes of stepping outside. Accept it and pack lighter.

"The guests who enjoy themselves most are usually the ones who packed light and arrived ready to adapt. The ones who packed for every possible scenario spend half their trip managing their luggage." — Dre

One Thing Most People Forget

Cash in small denominations. The DR runs largely on cash for everyday transactions — colmados, local restaurants, taxis, market stalls, tips. ATMs are available in Juan Dolio and widely available in Santo Domingo, but having some pesos ready when you arrive saves the first-day scramble. Change is often scarce, so small bills are genuinely useful. Ask at the guest house when you arrive — I'll tell you the current exchange rate and the best place to change money locally.

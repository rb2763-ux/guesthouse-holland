Living Through Dominican History: 30+ Years of Change

By Dre (André) | ECO-Tourguide #001 March 13, 2026 7 min read

Most tourists visit the Dominican Republic for a week and think they understand it. I've lived here for over 30 years, and I'm still learning something new every day. The transformation I've witnessed isn't just history — it's a masterclass in resilience, adaptation, and the unstoppable spirit of the Dominican people.

The 1990s: A Country Finding Its Feet

When I first arrived in the early 1990s, the Dominican Republic was a very different place. Tourism was still in its infancy, concentrated mainly in Puerto Plata and Santo Domingo. The all-inclusive resort boom hadn't yet transformed Punta Cana from fishing villages into the tourism powerhouse it is today.

Power outages were a daily reality. The infamous "apagones" could last 8-12 hours a day in some areas. Every home had candles, every business had a backup plan. The sound of generators became as familiar as the morning roosters.

But even then, you could see the potential. The natural beauty was undeniable, the people were warm and welcoming, and there was an entrepreneurial energy that told me this country was going places.

The 2000s: The Tourism Boom

The turn of the millennium marked a turning point. International investment poured in, particularly from Spanish hotel chains. Punta Cana exploded from a handful of resorts to dozens. Samaná was "discovered" by European tourists. Las Terrenas transformed from a fishing village to a cosmopolitan beach town.

Infrastructure improved dramatically. The electricity situation stabilized (though apagones still happen occasionally). Roads were paved, airports expanded, telecommunications modernized. The country was becoming more connected, both internally and with the world.

I watched small beach shacks become successful restaurants. Local families who once fished for a living now owned tour companies. The Dominican middle class was growing, and with it, a new sense of possibility.

Hurricane Economics: Lessons in Resilience

Living through multiple hurricanes teaches you something about the Dominican character. I've seen communities devastated by storms — houses flattened, crops destroyed, infrastructure gone — only to rebuild stronger within months.

The 1998 Hurricane Georges was particularly devastating, killing hundreds and causing billions in damage. Yet within a year, you could barely tell a catastrophe had occurred. This wasn't government efficiency (though aid helped) — this was the Dominican people simply refusing to stay down.

I learned a valuable lesson: Dominicans don't wait for permission to rebuild their lives. They just do it.

Cultural Evolution: From Merengue to Dembow

Music is the heartbeat of Dominican culture, and watching it evolve has been fascinating. Merengue was — and still is — king, but in the 2000s, bachata underwent a renaissance. What was once considered "música de campo" (country music) went global, thanks to artists like Romeo Santos and Prince Royce.

Then came dembow and reggaeton, transforming every colmado (corner store) into a spontaneous dance party. The Dominican music scene became one of the most innovative in Latin America, blending traditional rhythms with modern sounds.

What hasn't changed? The passion. Walk through any neighborhood on a Saturday night, and you'll hear music pouring from every window, see families dancing in their living rooms. Music isn't entertainment here — it's oxygen.

Economic Transformation: From Agriculture to Services

When I arrived, the Dominican economy was heavily agricultural — sugar, coffee, cacao, tobacco. Today, tourism and free-trade zones (maquiladoras) dominate. The country has one of the fastest-growing economies in Latin America, though wealth distribution remains a challenge.

The rise of the middle class has been remarkable. Where once there were dirt roads and zinc-roof homes, now there are gated communities with swimming pools. Shopping malls rival anything in Miami. The Dominican Republic is no longer just a tourist destination — it's a place where people build careers and futures.

Yet traditional ways persist. Drive an hour outside Santo Domingo, and you'll still find communities living much as they did 50 years ago, farming by hand, cooking over wood fires. The contrast is stark, sometimes uncomfortable, but it's part of what makes this country so fascinating.

The Dark Side: Crime, Corruption, and Inequality

Honesty requires acknowledging the shadows. The Dominican Republic has its problems — political corruption, crime in certain areas, significant economic inequality. These aren't tourist brochure topics, but they're part of the real story.

I've watched neighborhoods gentrify, displacing long-time residents. I've seen beautiful beaches privatized by resort developers. I've witnessed the environmental cost of rapid development — deforestation, pollution, overfishing.

Progress always has a price, and the Dominican Republic is paying it. The question is whether future generations will decide it was worth it.

The COVID-19 Reality Check

The pandemic hit the Dominican Republic hard — not primarily from the virus itself, but from the economic devastation of closed borders. A country where 20% of GDP comes from tourism suddenly had empty hotels, unemployed workers, and a tourism industry on life support.

Yet once again, resilience prevailed. The country was among the first in the region to reopen to tourists, implementing health protocols that became a model for others. By 2022, tourism had largely recovered, though the scars remain for many small businesses that didn't survive.

The pandemic also accelerated changes that were already underway — remote work bringing digital nomads, increased interest in eco-tourism and sustainable practices, a growing awareness that the old model of mass tourism has limits.

What the Future Holds

After 30+ years, I'm cautiously optimistic about the Dominican Republic's future. The challenges are real — climate change, economic inequality, maintaining authentic culture in the face of globalization. But this country has overcome so much already.

What gives me hope? The young generation. I see Dominican teenagers coding websites, creating YouTube channels, starting eco-tour companies. They're connected to the world but proud of their roots. They want progress but not at the cost of their identity.

The Dominican Republic I first visited in the 1990s barely resembles the one I live in today. And the country in 2056 will probably be unrecognizable from this one. But I'm confident that same warm spirit, that unbreakable resilience, that passion for life — those will endure.

My Perspective as a Long-Term Resident

Living here for three decades has taught me that understanding a place takes time. Tourists see the beaches and resorts. Expats often form bubbles that insulate them from real Dominican life. But when you truly commit to a place — learn the language fluently, build genuine friendships, experience both the triumphs and struggles — you earn a perspective that's rare and valuable.

I'm not Dominican. I'll always be the Dutch guy who chose this island as home. But I've lived enough of its history to have opinions, to understand nuances, to see beyond the postcard version.

And what I've learned is this: The Dominican Republic is a country of contrasts, contradictions, and constant change. It will frustrate you and amaze you, often in the same day. But if you give it a chance, really get to know it, this place will change you. It certainly changed me.

Santo Domingo in a Day: Dre's Guide to the Capital

By Dre · May 26, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

Santo Domingo is forty minutes from Juan Dolio. I've made that drive hundreds of times. Here is what I'd show you if we went together.

Most guests who stay with me at Guest House Holland spend their days on the beach or out on the water, and that's exactly right — that's what Juan Dolio is for. But at some point during a longer stay, the question comes up: should we go to Santo Domingo? The answer is yes, once, and done properly it will be one of the best days of your trip.

Santo Domingo is the oldest continuously inhabited European city in the Americas. The Colonial Zone — the original walled city founded in 1498 — is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and it earns that designation. Walking its streets is a genuinely different experience from anywhere else in the Caribbean. The history here is not reconstructed or themed; it's simply present, built into the walls and the cobblestones and the churches that have been standing for five hundred years.

Getting There from Juan Dolio

The drive from Juan Dolio to Santo Domingo takes between 35 and 50 minutes depending on traffic, via the Autopista del Este. A taxi from the guest house to the Colonial Zone costs around 1,200–1,500 pesos each way — agree the price before you get in. Alternatively, a guagua (shared minibus) runs along the main road for a fraction of the cost, but it's slower and requires knowing where to get off.

I recommend leaving by 8:30 in the morning. The Colonial Zone is at its best in the early hours, before the heat builds and before the tour groups arrive. By midday it's crowded and hot; by 4pm it's pleasant again. Plan your day around that rhythm.

💡 Dre's Tip: Park — or have your taxi drop you — near the Puerta de la Misericordia, the old city gate on the western edge of the Colonial Zone. Start your walk from there and work eastward toward the sea. You'll cover the most important sites in a natural sequence and end up near the Malecón for lunch.

The Colonial Zone: What to See

The Catedral Primada de América — the first cathedral built in the New World — is the obvious starting point. Construction began in 1514 and it took decades to complete; the result is a building that mixes Gothic, Baroque, and Renaissance elements in a way that shouldn't work but does. Go inside. The interior is cool and quiet and the light through the windows is extraordinary in the morning.

From the cathedral, the Calle Las Damas runs north along the old city wall. This is the oldest paved street in the Americas, and it's lined with colonial buildings that have been restored to something close to their original state. The Fortaleza Ozama, at the northern end, is the oldest European military fortress in the Americas — the views from the tower over the Ozama River are worth the small entrance fee.

"I've walked Calle Las Damas more times than I can count. It still stops me. There's a quality of light in the early morning, when the street is empty and the stone is still cool, that I've never found anywhere else." — Dre

The Alcázar de Colón — the palace built for Diego Columbus, son of Christopher — sits on a plaza overlooking the river. The building has been carefully restored and the interior gives a genuine sense of how the colonial elite lived in the sixteenth century. It's worth an hour of your time.

Where to Eat

By midday you'll be ready to sit down. The Malecón — the seafront boulevard that runs along the southern edge of the city — has a string of restaurants that serve good Dominican food at reasonable prices. La Cafetera Colonial on Calle El Conde is a local institution that has been serving breakfast and lunch since the 1940s; the sancocho (a thick meat and vegetable stew) is the thing to order if it's on the menu.

Avoid the tourist-facing restaurants immediately around the cathedral plaza — the food is mediocre and the prices are inflated. Walk one block in any direction and you'll find better options at half the cost.

💡 Dre's Tip: Order a cold Presidente with your lunch. The beer is brewed in the DR and it's the right drink for the climate and the food. If you're not a beer drinker, a fresh-squeezed jugo de chinola (passion fruit juice) is the local alternative and it's excellent.

The Afternoon: Mercado Modelo and the Drive Back

After lunch, the Mercado Modelo on Avenida Mella is worth an hour if you're interested in local crafts, larimar jewellery (larimar is a blue stone found only in the DR), or Dominican rum to take home. Bargaining is expected and part of the experience — start at about half the asking price and work from there.

By 3pm the worst of the heat is passing and the Colonial Zone becomes pleasant again for a final walk. Head back toward the Puerta de la Misericordia and pick up your taxi for the return to Juan Dolio. You'll be back at the guest house in time for a swim before sunset — which is, as it should be, how the day ends.

"Santo Domingo is a city that rewards attention. Most tourists see the cathedral and the main plaza and think they've seen it. The real city is in the side streets, the small plazas, the conversations at the colmado on the corner. Give it a full day and it will give you something back." — Dre

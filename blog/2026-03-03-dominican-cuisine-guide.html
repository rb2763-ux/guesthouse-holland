A Taste of the Island: Dominican Cuisine Through Local Eyes

By Dre (André) | March 3, 2026 | 6 min read

← Back to Blog

After more than thirty years of calling the Dominican Republic home, I've eaten my way through countless kitchens, from five-star restaurants to roadside comedores where the plastic chairs wobble and the flavors sing. Dominican cuisine tells the story of this island—a beautiful fusion of Taíno, Spanish, and African influences that creates something uniquely delicious.

As the country's first certified ECO-Tourguide (#001), I've always believed that understanding a place means understanding its food. Let me take you on a culinary journey through the dishes that have become as familiar to me as the tropical breeze.

La Bandera: The Heart of Dominican Cooking

"La Bandera Dominicana"—the Dominican Flag—isn't just a symbol of the nation. It's also the name of the most beloved daily meal: white rice, red beans, and meat (usually stewed chicken or beef), often accompanied by salad and fried plantains. You'll find this served in homes across the country around noon, and it's the backbone of Dominican nutrition.

The secret is in the beans. "Habichuelas guisadas" are simmered slowly with sofrito—a fragrant base of onions, peppers, garlic, and cilantro. When done right, they're creamy, savory, and absolutely addictive. I've had La Bandera thousands of times, and I never tire of it.

Mangú: Breakfast of Champions

If you stay at Guest House Holland, you'll likely wake up to the smell of mangú—mashed green plantains, buttery and slightly sweet, topped with sautéed red onions. Paired with fried cheese, salami, and eggs (the famous "Los Tres Golpes"), it's the traditional Dominican breakfast that fuels farmers, fishermen, and tourists alike.

"The first time I tried mangú in 1994, I knew I'd found something special. Thirty years later, I still crave it every morning."

Seafood on the North Coast

Living near Sosúa and Cabarete, we're blessed with some of the freshest seafood in the Caribbean. Here are my favorite dishes:

  • Pescado con coco: Fish simmered in coconut milk, a specialty of Samaná that has spread across the North Coast
  • Camarones al ajillo: Shrimp in garlic butter, simple but perfect when the catch is fresh
  • Lambi: Conch, prepared grilled, in salads, or as ceviche—a true Caribbean delicacy
  • Pulpo: Octopus, often served in a tangy vinaigrette with onions and peppers

For the best seafood experience, I always recommend the small family restaurants in Puerto Plata's Malecón or the beachside spots in Sosúa. The fish that was swimming that morning ends up on your plate by noon.

Street Food Adventures

Some of the best Dominican food comes from the humblest places. Keep an eye out for:

  • Chimichurri: Dominican-style burgers loaded with cabbage, tomatoes, and a special sauce (not to be confused with the Argentine chimichurri)
  • Empanadas: Crispy fried turnovers filled with beef, chicken, or cheese
  • Yaroa: A newer creation—layers of meat, cheese, and sauce over fries or plantains
  • Pica Pollo: Dominican fried chicken, marinated in lime and spices, impossibly crispy

Tropical Fruits You Must Try

The fruit here is unlike anything you've tasted back home. At 80 years old, I still get excited when certain fruits come into season:

  • Chinola (passion fruit): Tart and aromatic, perfect as juice
  • Lechosa (papaya): Sweet and soft, eaten fresh or in batidas (smoothies)
  • Guanábana (soursop): Creamy and unique, beloved in juices and ice cream
  • Mamey: A rare treat with orange flesh and complex sweetness

Where to Eat: Dre's Recommendations

After three decades, I have my favorites. When you visit, ask me for current recommendations—restaurants come and go, but I keep track of who's cooking with love and who's just going through the motions.

My general advice: eat where the Dominicans eat. If a restaurant is full of locals at lunchtime, that's your best indicator of quality. Don't be afraid of the simple comedores with their daily specials written on chalkboards. Some of the best meals of my life have come from places with no menus at all.

"Food is connection. Every meal in the Dominican Republic is an invitation to slow down, share with others, and appreciate the abundance of this beautiful island."

30 Years of Local Secrets: What Tourists Miss in the Dominican Republic

By André · February 24, 2026 · 6 min read

After living in the Dominican Republic for over three decades, I've watched millions of tourists come and go. Most leave having seen only 10% of what this beautiful country has to offer. Here are the secrets I wish someone had told me when I first arrived.

The Best Time to Visit (That Nobody Talks About)

Everyone flocks to the DR between December and April, and yes, the weather is spectacular. But here's what locals know: September and October, the so-called "hurricane season," are often the most magical months. The crowds disappear, prices drop by 40-50%, and despite the name, actual hurricanes are rare. The afternoon showers last 20 minutes and leave behind the most spectacular double rainbows you'll ever see.

In my 30+ years here, I've only had to postpone a tour twice due to weather. Meanwhile, I've given hundreds of tours in "hurricane season" under perfect skies.

André's Tip

Book your trip for late September. You'll have the beaches practically to yourself, and hotel rates are at their lowest. Just pack a light rain jacket for those brief afternoon showers.

Where the Locals Actually Eat

I see tourists paying $30 for mediocre pasta at beachfront restaurants when the best food in the country costs $3 at a "comedor" – a small family-run eatery. The secret? Look for the places where Dominican families are eating. No English menu? Perfect. That's where you want to be.

Order "la bandera" (the flag) – rice, beans, meat, and salad. It's the national dish, and every grandmother makes it differently. My favorite comedor has been run by the same family for 40 years. The señora still cooks everything herself, and the moro (rice with beans) is worth driving an hour for.

For seafood, skip Boca Chica's tourist strip. Drive 15 minutes east to the fishing villages where the catch comes in every morning. You'll pay a quarter of the price for fish that was swimming three hours ago.

The Hidden Beaches Nobody Knows

Punta Cana is beautiful, but it's also a manicured resort experience. If you want the DR I fell in love with, you need to go where the roads get rough.

Near Juan Dolio, there's a stretch of coastline that doesn't appear on Google Maps. You have to know a fisherman to find the path. The beach is empty, the water is turquoise, and local families bring their Sunday picnics there. I've taken guests who've told me it was the highlight of their entire trip.

Another secret: the south coast. Everyone goes to the north (Puerto Plata, Samaná) or the east (Punta Cana). But the south – Barahona, Pedernales, the Bahía de las Águilas – is the DR's last frontier. No all-inclusives. No tourist buses. Just untouched nature and the warmest people you'll ever meet.

What I Wish I'd Known 30 Years Ago

When I arrived in the Dominican Republic as a young man from Holland, I thought I knew everything. I was wrong. Here are the lessons that took decades to learn:

Slow down. "Ahora" (now) means "sometime today." "Mañana" (tomorrow) means "eventually." Fighting this will make you miserable. Embracing it will change your life. The Dominican approach to time isn't laziness – it's a different philosophy. Relationships matter more than schedules. A conversation with a neighbor is more important than being somewhere on time.

Learn basic Spanish. Yes, you can get by with English in tourist areas. But the real Dominican Republic opens up when you can chat with the grandmother selling mangoes by the roadside. Even broken Spanish is appreciated and rewarded.

The people are the treasure. The beaches are beautiful, the mountains are stunning, but what kept me here for 30 years is the warmth of the Dominican people. Accept invitations. Join the domino games. Dance merengue badly. You'll leave with friendships that last a lifetime.

My Promise

Every tour I lead shows you the Dominican Republic I've come to love – not the brochure version. We visit the places where I've made friends over decades, where they know my name and will welcome you like family.

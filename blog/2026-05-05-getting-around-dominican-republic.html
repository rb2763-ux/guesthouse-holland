Getting Around the Dominican Republic: What Nobody Tells You

By Dre · May 5, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

30+ years living in the Dominican Republic. I've travelled every road in this country — by guagua, motoconcho, taxi, and everything in between. Here's what I've learned.

Most visitors to the Dominican Republic arrive at an airport, get into a transfer bus, and spend their entire holiday within walking distance of their hotel. They see the resort, the beach, and maybe one organised excursion. They go home having experienced about five percent of what this country actually is.

I understand why. Getting around the DR looks complicated from the outside. The transport system is informal, the roads are variable, and nobody hands you a map at the airport with clear instructions. But after thirty years of living here, I can tell you that once you understand how it works, getting around the Dominican Republic is not only manageable — it's one of the most interesting parts of being here. This is what I'd tell you before you set off.

The Guagua: The Backbone of Dominican Transport

The guagua (pronounced gwah-gwah) is a minibus — usually a Toyota HiAce or similar — that runs fixed routes between towns and cities. It is how the majority of Dominicans travel, and it is extraordinarily cheap. A guagua from Juan Dolio to Santo Domingo costs around 80 pesos — less than two dollars. The same journey in a taxi costs ten times that.

Guaguas don't run to a timetable. They leave when they're full, which in practice means you wait at the roadside, flag one down, squeeze in, and go. They stop frequently to pick up and drop off passengers. The journey takes longer than a taxi, the seats are close together, and the music is always on. It is also a genuine window into daily Dominican life that no resort excursion can replicate.

"I still take the guagua when I'm going somewhere local. You hear conversations, you see the towns you pass through, you understand the country in a way that's impossible from a private car. It's not always comfortable, but it's always real." — Dre

To catch a guagua from Juan Dolio, stand on the main road (Autopista del Este) and wave at any passing minibus. Tell the driver or the cobrador (the person who collects fares) where you're going. They'll tell you if they're heading that way. Pay when you arrive or when the cobrador asks — usually somewhere in the middle of the journey.

The Motoconcho: Fast, Cheap, and Entirely Your Decision

A motoconcho is a motorcycle taxi. They are everywhere — outside supermarkets, at street corners, near the beach — and they are the fastest way to get around town. A short ride within Juan Dolio costs 50 to 100 pesos. You sit on the back, hold on, and arrive at your destination in minutes.

I will not tell you that motoconchos are completely without risk — they are motorcycles in a country where traffic can be unpredictable, and helmets are not always offered. What I will tell you is that Dominicans use them constantly, including families with children, and that for short distances in daylight they are a perfectly normal way to get around. Use your judgment. If the driver seems reckless before you've even got on, find another one.

💡 Dre's Tip: Always agree on the price before you get on a motoconcho. Ask "¿Cuánto?" (how much?) and confirm the destination clearly. Most drivers are honest, but agreeing upfront avoids any confusion at the end of the ride.

Taxis: Comfortable but Negotiate First

Taxis in the Dominican Republic are not metered. The price is negotiated before you get in, and if you don't negotiate, you will pay the tourist price, which is significantly higher than what a local would pay. This is not dishonesty — it's simply how the system works, and both sides understand it.

Before getting into any taxi, agree on the destination and the price. Say the destination clearly, ask "¿Cuánto cuesta?" and be prepared to negotiate gently if the first number seems high. Having a rough idea of what the journey should cost helps — ask at the guest house before you go anywhere, and I'll give you current prices for common routes.

For longer journeys — to Santo Domingo, to the airport, to Boca Chica — a taxi is often the most practical option, especially with luggage or in a group. Split between three or four people, the cost becomes reasonable and the comfort is considerably better than a guagua.

Rental Cars: Freedom With Caveats

Renting a car gives you the freedom to explore the country at your own pace, and the DR rewards exploration — there are parts of this island that most tourists never see because they require a car to reach. If you're planning to travel beyond the immediate area, a rental is worth considering.

A few honest observations from someone who has driven these roads for decades. Dominican driving culture is assertive. Lane markings are treated as suggestions. Speed bumps — called tumulos — appear without warning and can be severe. The roads between major towns are generally good; the roads in rural areas are not always. And driving at night, particularly outside of towns, requires extra caution.

💡 Dre's Tip: If you rent a car, get full insurance and photograph every existing scratch before you drive away. Keep your rental documents and your passport copy in the car. And if you're pulled over by police, stay calm, be polite, and have your documents ready. It's rarely a serious situation.

The Carrito: For Short Hops Between Towns

Between Juan Dolio and nearby towns like Guayacanes or La Romana, you'll find carritos — shared taxis that run fixed routes for a fixed price. They work similarly to guaguas but are usually regular cars rather than minibuses. Flag one down on the main road, confirm the destination, and pay the standard fare when you arrive. It's typically 50 to 150 pesos depending on the distance.

Getting to Santo Domingo

Santo Domingo is about 45 minutes from Juan Dolio by car, or an hour to an hour and a half by guagua depending on traffic. The capital is worth a visit — the Colonial Zone is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and genuinely impressive, and the city has restaurants, museums, and a cultural life that the beach towns don't offer.

The easiest way to go is by guagua from the main road, changing at the terminal in the east of the city and taking a taxi or the Metro to wherever you're going in the centre. The Santo Domingo Metro is clean, cheap, and easy to use — a single journey costs 20 pesos. It doesn't cover the whole city, but it gets you to the Colonial Zone without the traffic.

"The first time most visitors take a guagua, they're nervous. By the third time, they wonder why they ever paid for a taxi. The country opens up completely once you understand how to move around it." — Dre

A Final Word

The Dominican Republic is not a difficult country to navigate once you stop expecting it to work like home. The transport system is informal, flexible, and built around the way people actually live here. It rewards patience, a little Spanish, and a willingness to figure things out as you go.

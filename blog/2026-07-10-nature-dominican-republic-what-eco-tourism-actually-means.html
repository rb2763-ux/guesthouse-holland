🌿 Nature & Eco-Tourism

Nature in the DR: What Eco-Tourism Actually Means

By Dre · July 10, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've watched this country's natural world change over thirty years. Some of it has recovered. Some of it hasn't. Here's what I think about eco-tourism — and what the word actually means when you strip away the marketing.

The Dominican Republic is one of the most biologically diverse countries in the Caribbean. It has cloud forests, dry forests, mangroves, coral reefs, alpine meadows, and wetlands — often within a few hours of each other. Pico Duarte, at just over 3,000 metres, is the highest peak in the entire Caribbean. The country shares the island of Hispaniola with Haiti, and the contrast between the two sides — one heavily forested, one largely deforested — is visible from space.

That natural richness is real. What gets called "eco-tourism" here is sometimes real too. And sometimes it isn't.

What the Label Often Means

In the Dominican Republic, as in most places, "eco-tourism" has become a marketing term as much as a description. A resort can call itself eco-friendly because it has solar panels on the roof and a recycling bin in the lobby. A tour can call itself an eco-tour because it goes into a forest. Neither of these things is necessarily wrong, but neither tells you much about whether the experience actually benefits the natural environment or the communities that live in it.

The questions worth asking are simpler: Does the money stay local? Does the activity disturb what it claims to protect? Is the group size small enough that the place can absorb it? Is there anything educational happening, or is it just scenery?

"I became an ECO-Tourguide because I wanted a word for what I was already doing — taking small groups to places that matter, explaining why they matter, and leaving them exactly as we found them. The certification came later. The practice came first." — Dre

Where the Nature Is Actually Worth Seeing

Los Haitises National Park, on the north coast of the Samaná Bay, is one of the most extraordinary landscapes in the Caribbean. Limestone karst formations rise from the water like green islands. The mangroves are dense and healthy. The caves contain Taíno pictographs that are thousands of years old. You reach it by boat, which keeps the numbers manageable. Go with a guide who knows the ecology, not just the route.

Lago Enriquillo, in the southwest, is a hypersaline lake below sea level — the lowest point in the Caribbean — and home to the largest wild population of American crocodiles in the world. Flamingos feed in the shallows. Rhinoceros iguanas sun themselves on the banks. It is genuinely strange and genuinely wild. The southwest is the least-visited region of the country, which is exactly why it's worth going.

The Cordillera Central — the mountain range that runs through the middle of the country — has cloud forest at altitude, rivers cold enough to swim in, and a pace of life that has nothing to do with the coast. Jarabacoa is the base for most mountain activities: hiking, rafting, canyoning. The landscape is green in a way that surprises people who only know the DR from beach photos.

🌿 ECO Note: The Dominican Republic has designated roughly 30% of its territory as protected areas — one of the highest proportions in the Caribbean. In practice, enforcement varies. The most protected places are the ones that are hardest to reach. That's not a coincidence.

The Mangroves Near Juan Dolio

People staying in Juan Dolio often don't realise that there are mangrove systems within easy reach. Mangroves are among the most productive ecosystems on the planet — they filter water, protect coastlines from storm surge, and provide nursery habitat for fish that end up on restaurant plates across the Caribbean. They are also among the most threatened.

I take small groups into the mangroves by kayak when conditions allow. It's quiet, it's slow, and it shows people something they wouldn't see from a beach chair. The birds alone are worth it — herons, egrets, frigatebirds, the occasional kingfisher. You don't need to go far to find something worth paying attention to.

💡 Dre's Tip: The best time for any nature experience in the DR is early morning. Animals are active, the light is good, and the heat hasn't arrived yet. If a tour starts at 10am, it's probably not optimised for wildlife — it's optimised for people who want to sleep in. Both are valid. Just know which one you're booking.

What Responsible Visiting Looks Like

Stay on marked trails. Don't touch coral. Don't feed wildlife. Don't take shells, rocks, or plants from protected areas. These are not complicated rules, but they matter at scale — when thousands of people visit the same place, small individual actions add up.

Support local guides and local businesses where you can. The economic argument for conservation is the strongest one in most developing countries: if the forest is worth more standing than cut, it stays standing. Tourism that puts money into local hands makes that argument real.

And if you're not sure whether something is genuinely eco-friendly or just labelled that way — ask. A good operator will be able to tell you exactly what they do and why. If they can't, that's your answer.

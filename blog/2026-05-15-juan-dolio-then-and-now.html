Juan Dolio Then and Now: What 30 Years of Change Looks Like

By Dre · May 15, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I arrived in Juan Dolio more than thirty years ago. The place I found then and the place you're visiting now are the same stretch of coastline — but almost everything else has changed. Here's what I remember.

When people ask me what Juan Dolio was like when I first arrived, I tell them to imagine the beach as it is now — the same sand, the same sea, the same light in the afternoon — and then remove almost everything else. No resort hotels along the coast road. No supermarkets. No reliable electricity after dark. A handful of colmados, a few fishing families, and a road that turned to mud when it rained.

That's not a complaint. It was a different kind of place, and it had a quality that's harder to find now — a genuine quietness, a sense that you had arrived somewhere that the wider world hadn't yet noticed. I noticed it, which is why I stayed.

The Beach in the Early Days

The beach itself hasn't changed as much as you might expect. The sand is the same fine white sand it always was. The water is the same temperature, the same colour, the same clarity. The reef offshore is still there, though it has had its difficult years and its recoveries. What has changed is everything around it.

In the early years, the beach was used almost entirely by local families on weekends and by the occasional adventurous traveller who had heard about it through word of mouth. There were no beach bars in the modern sense — just a man with a cooler selling cold Presidentes, and sometimes a woman cooking fish on a grill she'd brought from home. You ate what was available, and what was available was always fresh.

"The first time I swam here, there was no one else on the beach. Just the sea, the reef, and the sound of the wind in the palms. I thought: this is the place. I was right." — Dre

How the Town Grew

Juan Dolio grew in stages, and not always in the directions people expected. The first wave of development came in the late 1980s and early 1990s, when a few all-inclusive resorts opened along the coast road. They brought infrastructure — paved roads, more reliable electricity, a proper water supply — but they also brought a different kind of visitor, one who arrived at the airport, transferred directly to the resort, and left a week later without having seen much of the actual country.

The second wave was different. It brought smaller guesthouses, independent restaurants, and a community of longer-term residents — Europeans, North Americans, Dominicans from the capital — who wanted to live here rather than just visit. That's the wave I was part of, and it's the community that gave Juan Dolio much of its character.

The town today is a mixture of all of these layers. You have the all-inclusive resorts at one end of the spectrum and the local fishing families at the other, with everything in between — guesthouses, apartments, restaurants that serve both tourists and locals, a market that operates on its own schedule regardless of what the guidebooks say.

💡 Dre's Tip: The parts of Juan Dolio that feel most authentic are the parts that haven't changed much — the side streets away from the coast road, the colmados where locals buy their groceries, the beach at the far end of town where the fishing boats still go out before dawn. Walk in those directions and you'll find the place I arrived in, still there underneath everything that's been built on top of it.

What the Sea Has Taught Me

Living on this coast for thirty years means watching the sea in all its moods. I've seen hurricanes come through and reshape the beach overnight. I've watched the reef bleach in hot years and recover in cooler ones. I've seen fish populations decline and, in some areas, come back when the fishing pressure eased. The sea here is not static — it's a living system, and it responds to everything that happens around it.

That's part of why I became an ECO-Tourguide. Not because I had a particular ideology about it, but because I'd watched long enough to understand that the way you treat a place determines whether it's still worth visiting in twenty years. The tourists who come here now are inheriting something that was built up over a long time. How they treat it determines what gets passed on.

"I've seen this reef at its worst — bleached white, almost silent. And I've seen it come back. The ocean has more resilience than people give it credit for. But it needs help, not just good intentions." — Dre

What Has Stayed the Same

The things that haven't changed are the things that matter most. The light here in the late afternoon, when the sun drops toward the hills and turns everything gold — that's the same light I saw on my first evening. The sound of the sea at night, when the wind drops and the waves come in slow and steady — that's the same sound. The warmth of the water in the morning, when you're the first person in and the surface is still flat — that hasn't changed either.

The people, too, have a continuity that development hasn't touched. The families who were fishing here when I arrived are still fishing. Their children grew up on this beach. Some of them work in the resorts now; others still go out in the same boats their fathers used. The community has adapted, as communities do, but it hasn't disappeared.

Why I'm Still Here

People ask me sometimes why I stayed. The honest answer is that I never found a reason to leave. The place changed around me, and some of those changes I welcomed and some I didn't, but the essential thing — the quality of the light, the temperature of the water, the particular character of this stretch of coast — that stayed constant. When you find a place that does that, you hold onto it.

Guest House Holland is my way of sharing it. When guests arrive and I show them the beach, I'm showing them the same beach I found thirty years ago. The context has changed. The beach hasn't. That's the thing worth coming for.

Best Beaches in the DR: Beyond the Postcard

By Dre · July 7, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

More than thirty years living on this coastline. I've watched beaches get discovered, get crowded, and occasionally get ruined. Here's what I actually think.

The Dominican Republic has roughly 1,600 kilometres of coastline. That is a lot of beach. Some of it is genuinely extraordinary. Some of it is overrun, overpriced, and surrounded by vendors who won't leave you alone. Knowing the difference before you arrive saves you a lot of disappointment.

I've been swimming in these waters for more than thirty years. Here is my honest assessment of what's worth your time — and what the travel brochures get wrong.

The Beaches That Live Up to the Hype

Playa Rincón, near Las Galeras on the Samaná Peninsula, is the closest thing to an unspoiled Caribbean beach that still exists in this country. No hotels on the beach, no jet skis, no vendors every ten metres. You get there by boat or by a rough road, which is exactly why it stays quiet. The water is clear, the palms are real, and you can spend a full day there without feeling like you're in a theme park. Go on a weekday if you can.

Playa Frontón, also near Las Galeras, is accessible only by boat or a serious hike. That keeps the numbers down. The cliffs behind the beach are dramatic, the snorkelling is good, and the whole place feels like it belongs to you. Bring everything you need — there's nothing there.

Bahía de las Águilas, in the far southwest near Pedernales, is the most remote major beach in the country. It takes effort to get there — a long drive on bad roads, then a boat — but the beach itself is ten kilometres of white sand with almost no one on it. The southwest is the least-visited part of the DR, and this beach is the reason to go.

"The best beaches in the Dominican Republic are the ones that cost you something to reach — time, effort, an early start. The ones that cost you nothing to reach are usually the ones everyone else is already at." — Dre

The Beaches That Are Overhyped

Bávaro and Punta Cana are fine beaches. The sand is white, the water is warm, the colour is right. But the experience of being there is largely the experience of being at a resort. The beach is divided into sections by hotel, the sunbeds are rented, and the vendors are persistent. If you're staying at an all-inclusive, you'll use the beach and it will be pleasant. If you're travelling independently and expecting something wild and empty, you'll be disappointed.

Cabarete is popular with kitesurfers and has a lively beach-bar scene. The wind is real and the kitesurfing is genuinely good. But the beach itself is not particularly beautiful, and the town has the slightly worn-out feel of a place that peaked a few years ago. Worth a day if you're in the north; not worth a special trip.

💡 Dre's Tip: Avoid any beach that has a large hotel directly on it between 10am and 3pm in high season. The ratio of people to sand becomes unfavourable very quickly. Go early, go late, or go somewhere else.

The Beaches Near Juan Dolio

Juan Dolio itself has a long stretch of beach that is calm, warm, and rarely crowded outside of Dominican holidays. The water is shallow for a long way out, which makes it good for swimming and bad for surfing. The reef offshore keeps the waves down. It's a comfortable beach rather than a spectacular one — good for a morning swim, good for watching the pelicans, good for doing nothing in particular.

Further east, toward Guayacanes and beyond, the beach gets quieter and the water stays clear. These are local beaches — Dominican families on weekends, almost empty on weekdays. No vendors, no sunbeds for rent, no one trying to sell you anything. Just beach.

The stretch between Juan Dolio and La Romana has some of the least-visited coastline in the southeast. There are places along that road where you can pull over, walk down to the water, and have the beach entirely to yourself. I know most of them. That's the kind of thing I show people on tours.

💡 Dre's Tip: The best time for any beach in the DR is before 9am. The light is different, the water is calmer, the vendors aren't set up yet, and you have the place to yourself. Bring coffee. Watch the fishermen come in. It's the best hour of the day.

A Word on Beach Safety

The Dominican Republic has strong currents in some areas, particularly on the north coast and around headlands. Rip currents exist. If you're not a confident swimmer, stay in the shallow water and pay attention to flags where they're posted. The Caribbean side (south and east) is generally calmer than the Atlantic side (north). Juan Dolio is on the Caribbean side — the water is gentle.

Petty theft on beaches happens, as it does everywhere. Don't leave valuables unattended. Don't bring anything to the beach that you'd be upset to lose. Common sense applies.

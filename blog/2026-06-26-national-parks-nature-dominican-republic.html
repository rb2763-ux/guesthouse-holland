Beyond the Beach: The Dominican Republic's National Parks and Natural Wonders

By Dre · June 26, 2026 · 7 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've been exploring this island's interior for more than thirty years. The beach is beautiful. But the mountains, the lakes, the caves — that's where the Dominican Republic shows you what it really is.

The Dominican Republic has thirty national parks and protected areas covering roughly a third of the country's total land area. Most visitors to the island never set foot in any of them. They arrive, they go to the beach, they eat at the resort, they leave. I understand it — the beaches here are genuinely extraordinary. But if that's all you see, you've missed most of the country.

I've been walking this island for more than thirty years. The interior is where the Dominican Republic surprises you. Here are the natural places I think every visitor should know about.

Los Haitises National Park

Los Haitises is one of the most unusual landscapes in the Caribbean. The park covers around 1,600 square kilometres of karst topography — a terrain of limestone hills, mangrove forests, and hidden caves that rise dramatically from Samaná Bay. The name comes from the Taíno word for "hilly land," and the hills here are unlike anything else on the island: rounded, densely forested mounds that emerge from the water like something from a prehistoric painting.

The caves are the highlight. Several contain Taíno pictographs — drawings left by the island's original inhabitants centuries before Columbus arrived. Seeing them in context, inside a cave that opens onto a mangrove bay, is a genuinely moving experience. The park is accessible by boat from Samaná or Sánchez, and the journey through the mangroves is worth the trip on its own.

💡 Dre's Tip: Go early — by eight in the morning if you can. The light in the mangroves at that hour is extraordinary, and you'll have the caves largely to yourself. By midday the tour boats arrive in numbers. A local guide is essential; the park is large and the best caves are not always obvious from the water.

Pico Duarte: The Highest Peak in the Caribbean

Pico Duarte stands at 3,098 metres — the highest point in the entire Caribbean. Most people who visit the Dominican Republic have no idea it exists. The mountain sits in the Cordillera Central, the spine of the island, and reaching the summit requires a two-to-three day hike through pine forests, cloud forest, and open páramo. The temperature at the top can drop below freezing at night.

I have made this climb several times over the years. It is not technically difficult — it is a long walk, not a technical climb — but it is demanding, and it requires preparation. The reward is a view that stretches, on a clear day, to the coast in multiple directions. You are standing on top of the Caribbean. There is nothing quite like it.

"People are always surprised when I tell them the Dominican Republic has mountains with pine forests and cold nights. They think of the beach. But this island has everything — desert, rainforest, alpine meadow, mangrove. It's one of the most ecologically diverse places on earth." — Dre

Lago Enriquillo: A Salt Lake Below Sea Level

Lago Enriquillo is the largest lake in the Caribbean and one of the lowest points in the Western Hemisphere — sitting about 40 metres below sea level. It is a salt lake, home to American crocodiles, flamingos, rhinoceros iguanas, and a remarkable variety of birds. The landscape around it is arid and otherworldly, more like the Galápagos than the Caribbean coast most visitors imagine.

The lake has been growing significantly over the past two decades — a consequence of changing rainfall patterns that has flooded surrounding communities and farmland. Visiting it now, you see both the extraordinary natural environment and the real consequences of climate change on a small island nation. It is not a comfortable visit in that sense, but it is an important one.

💡 Dre's Tip: The boat trips to Isla Cabritos — the island in the middle of the lake where the crocodiles and iguanas are most concentrated — run in the morning. Bring water, sun protection, and a hat. The heat around the lake is intense and there is almost no shade on the island itself.

The Samaná Peninsula

The Samaná Peninsula in the northeast is where the Dominican Republic's natural beauty is most concentrated in a small area. Humpback whales arrive in Samaná Bay between January and March to breed — one of the most reliable whale-watching locations in the Atlantic. The peninsula also has some of the country's most beautiful waterfalls, including El Limón, which drops 52 metres into a natural pool in the middle of the forest.

The town of Las Terrenas on the north coast of the peninsula has a different character from the rest of the country — more European in feel, with a large expat community and excellent restaurants. It makes a good base for exploring the peninsula's natural attractions.

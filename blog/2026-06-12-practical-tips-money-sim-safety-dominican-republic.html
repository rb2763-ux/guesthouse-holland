Money, SIM Cards, and Staying Safe in the DR: Dre's Practical Guide

By Dre · June 12, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've been helping visitors navigate the Dominican Republic for more than thirty years. The practical questions are always the same — here are the honest answers.

Every week I get the same questions from guests arriving at Guest House Holland. How do I get Dominican pesos? Is my card going to work? Which SIM card should I buy? Is it safe to walk around at night? These are sensible questions and they deserve straight answers — not the vague reassurances you find in most travel guides.

Here is what I tell every guest when they arrive. Thirty years of living here has taught me what actually matters and what visitors worry about unnecessarily.

Money: What You Need to Know

The Dominican peso (DOP) is the currency and you will need it for almost everything outside the resort hotels. Markets, local restaurants, colmados (corner shops), taxis, and most small businesses operate in pesos. Some tourist-facing businesses quote prices in US dollars, but paying in pesos at the local rate is almost always cheaper.

The best exchange rates are at the banco exchange windows inside supermarkets and at dedicated casas de cambio (exchange offices). The rate at the airport is significantly worse — change only what you need to get into town, then find a proper exchange office. Banks also exchange currency but the queues can be long and the process slow.

💡 Dre's Tip: The Banco Popular and BanReservas ATMs are the most reliable for international cards. Use ATMs attached to bank branches rather than standalone machines in tourist areas — the fees are lower and the machines are better maintained. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently to minimise transaction fees.

Credit and debit cards are accepted at larger supermarkets, hotels, and some restaurants, but many local businesses are cash only. Always carry some pesos. Running out of cash in a local market with no ATM nearby is a genuinely inconvenient situation that is easy to avoid.

"I always tell guests: bring more cash than you think you need for the first two days. Once you know where the ATMs are and how the exchange works, you can manage easily. But arriving without pesos on a Sunday evening when the banks are closed is a bad start." — Dre

SIM Cards: Get One at the Airport

Mobile data in the Dominican Republic is cheap and the coverage is good along the coast and in the cities. The two main providers are Claro and Altice. Both sell prepaid SIM cards at the airport arrivals hall — buy one before you leave the terminal.

A prepaid SIM with a data package costs around 500–800 pesos (roughly $8–14 USD) and will give you several gigabytes of data for a week or two. Top-up cards (recargas) are sold at almost every colmado and supermarket. The process is simple and the staff at the airport kiosks speak enough English to help you set it up.

💡 Dre's Tip: Claro has slightly better coverage in rural areas and along the coast east of Juan Dolio. Altice tends to be faster in the cities. For most visitors staying in the Juan Dolio area, either will work perfectly well. Don't overthink it — just buy whichever has the shorter queue at the airport.

Roaming on your home SIM is possible but expensive. A local prepaid SIM is almost always the better option for stays of more than a day or two.

Safety: The Honest Version

The Dominican Republic has a reputation for crime that is partly deserved and partly exaggerated. Like any country, it has areas that are safer than others and situations that are riskier than others. Juan Dolio is one of the quieter, more relaxed parts of the country — it is not Santo Domingo, and the concerns are different.

The most common problems visitors encounter are petty theft and opportunistic scams — not violent crime. Keep your phone in your pocket rather than in your hand when walking through busy areas. Don't leave valuables visible in a parked car. Be sceptical of strangers who approach you with unsolicited offers of tours, transport, or friendship.

💡 Dre's Tip: Walking around Juan Dolio during the day is perfectly safe. At night, stick to the main road and the areas you know. Use a taxi or moto-concho (motorcycle taxi) rather than walking long distances after dark — not because it's dangerous, but because it's more comfortable and costs almost nothing. A moto-concho ride within Juan Dolio costs 50–100 pesos.

The beach at night deserves a specific mention. The resort beaches with lighting and security are fine. Isolated stretches of beach after dark are not the place to be alone. This is true of beaches everywhere in the world, not just the DR.

The most important safety advice I give every guest is simple: use common sense, don't flash expensive items, and ask locals — including me — if you're unsure about a specific area or situation. Thirty years of living here means I know which concerns are real and which are not.

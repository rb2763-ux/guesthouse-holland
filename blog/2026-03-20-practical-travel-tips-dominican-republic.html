Practical Travel Tips for the Dominican Republic

By André de Broeders • ECO-Tourguide #001 • March 20, 2026

After more than 30 years living in the Dominican Republic, I've learned what travelers actually need to know—not the sanitized advice from guidebooks, but the real, practical information that makes the difference between a frustrating trip and a fantastic one.

Let me share the lessons I've learned, the mistakes I've made, and the tips that will help you navigate this beautiful, chaotic, wonderful country.

Money Matters

Currency and Exchange

The Dominican peso (DOP or RD$) is the local currency. As of 2026, the exchange rate hovers around 57-60 pesos per US dollar, but it fluctuates. Here's what you need to know:

  • Bring US dollars: They're widely accepted and get better exchange rates than euros or other currencies.
  • Use ATMs for the best rates: Airport exchange booths are convenient but expensive. ATMs give you the official rate.
  • Carry small bills: A 1000-peso note can be hard to break at small shops. Keep plenty of 100s and 200s.
  • Credit cards work in tourist areas: But many local places are cash-only. Always have pesos on hand.
Money Tip: The Banco Popular and Banco BHD ATMs are reliable and found everywhere. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently to minimize ATM fees. Most machines have a 10,000-peso limit per transaction.

Tipping Culture

Tipping is expected in the Dominican Republic, but it's different from the US:

  • Restaurants: 10% is standard (many add it automatically—check your bill).
  • Taxis: Round up or add 10-20 pesos for short rides.
  • Tour guides: 200-500 pesos per person, depending on the tour length.
  • Hotel staff: 50-100 pesos for housekeeping, 100-200 for bellhops.

Transportation

Getting Around

Transportation in the DR ranges from modern to adventurous. Here's the reality:

Taxis: Use Uber in Santo Domingo and Santiago—it's safer and cheaper than street taxis. In tourist areas, negotiate the fare before getting in. A typical ride within a town should be 200-400 pesos.

Guaguas (local buses): Cheap (25-50 pesos) but crowded and confusing for tourists. They're an adventure if you're feeling brave.

Motoconchos (motorcycle taxis): Fast, cheap (50-100 pesos), and terrifying. I've used them for 30 years, but they're not for everyone. If you do, wear the helmet they offer.

Car rentals: Expensive (expect $40-80/day) and driving here is... intense. Dominican drivers are aggressive, traffic rules are suggestions, and potholes are everywhere. But if you want freedom to explore, it's worth it.

Transportation Tip: For longer distances, use Caribe Tours or Metro bus lines. They're comfortable, air-conditioned, and reliable. Book tickets a day ahead during holidays.

Safety and Common Sense

The Dominican Republic is generally safe for tourists, but like anywhere, you need to be smart:

  • Don't flash wealth: Leave expensive jewelry at home. Use a cheap phone for walking around.
  • Be aware of your surroundings: Especially in Santo Domingo and at night. Stick to well-lit, populated areas.
  • Avoid isolated beaches alone: Go with a group or a guide, especially to remote areas.
  • Watch your drinks: In bars and clubs, keep your drink with you. This is universal travel advice, but it applies here too.
  • Trust your instincts: If something feels off, it probably is. Walk away.

That said, I've lived here for 30+ years and never had a serious problem. Most Dominicans are friendly, helpful, and honest. Don't let fear ruin your trip—just be sensible.

Communication

Language

Spanish is the official language. In tourist areas, many people speak English, but outside those bubbles, you'll need Spanish. Learn these basics:

  • Buenos días (good morning), buenas tardes (good afternoon), buenas noches (good evening)
  • Por favor (please), gracias (thank you)
  • ¿Cuánto cuesta? (How much does it cost?)
  • No entiendo (I don't understand)
  • ¿Dónde está...? (Where is...?)

Even butchered Spanish is appreciated. Dominicans are patient with learners and will help you.

Internet and Phone

Buy a local SIM card at the airport or any Claro/Altice/Viva store. Data is cheap—1GB for about 200 pesos. Most hotels and restaurants have WiFi, but it's often slow.

Health and Hygiene

Water: Don't drink tap water. Buy bottled water (it's everywhere and cheap). Use it for brushing teeth too.

Food safety: Eat where locals eat. If a place is busy with Dominicans, the food is fresh. Avoid empty restaurants.

Mosquitoes: Bring repellent. Dengue and Zika are present, though not common. Use repellent at dawn and dusk.

Sun protection: The Caribbean sun is brutal. Wear sunscreen (SPF 50+), reapply often, and wear a hat. I still get burned after 30 years if I'm not careful.

Pharmacies: Well-stocked and many medications are available without prescription. Pharmacists are knowledgeable and helpful.

Health Tip: Bring basic medications from home (pain relievers, anti-diarrhea, antihistamines). They're available here but might be different brands or formulations.

Cultural Tips

Dominicans are warm and social: Don't be surprised if strangers strike up conversations. It's normal and friendly.

Time is flexible: "Dominican time" means things start late. A party at 8 PM? People arrive at 10. Relax and go with it.

Music is everywhere: Merengue, bachata, and reggaeton blast from cars, shops, and homes. It's part of the culture. Embrace it.

Dress modestly outside tourist areas: Dominicans dress well. Shorts and tank tops are fine at the beach, but cover up in towns and cities.

Haggling is expected at markets: But not in stores with price tags. At street markets, offer 60-70% of the asking price and negotiate from there.

What to Pack

After three decades, here's what I tell every visitor to bring:

  • Light, breathable clothing: Cotton and linen. It's hot and humid.
  • Comfortable walking shoes: Sidewalks are uneven. Flip-flops for the beach, real shoes for walking.
  • A light rain jacket: Tropical showers happen year-round.
  • Sunscreen and insect repellent: Expensive here, so bring from home.
  • A small daypack: For beach trips and excursions.
  • Copies of important documents: Passport, insurance, credit cards. Keep digital copies too.
  • A sense of humor: Things go wrong. Power outages happen. Plans change. Roll with it.

Best Time to Visit

December to April is peak season—dry, sunny, and perfect. But it's also crowded and expensive.

May to November is hurricane season, but hurricanes are rare. You'll get occasional rain, but also lower prices, fewer tourists, and lush green landscapes. I actually prefer this time.

Avoid Easter week (Semana Santa) unless you like crowds. The entire country goes on vacation.

Final Advice

The Dominican Republic isn't perfect. Infrastructure can be frustrating. Things don't always work as expected. But that's part of its charm.

The people are warm. The beaches are stunning. The food is delicious. The music is infectious. And if you approach it with patience, flexibility, and an open mind, you'll fall in love with this country just like I did 30 years ago.

"After 30+ years here, I still discover something new every week. The Dominican Republic rewards curiosity, patience, and a willingness to step outside your comfort zone. Come with an open heart, and you'll leave with memories that last a lifetime."

The Taíno: The First People of Hispaniola

By Dre · July 24, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

The longer you live in the Dominican Republic, the more you notice how much of the Taíno world is still here — in the language, the food, the place names, the way people relate to the land. Most visitors never look for it. That's a shame, because it's one of the most interesting layers of this island's story.

Before Columbus arrived in 1492, before the Spanish built their forts and churches, before the island was called Hispaniola — there were the Taíno. They had been living here for centuries, farming the land, fishing the coasts, building communities across the island they called Ayiti or Quisqueya. Both names are still used today: Haiti on the western side, and Quisqueya as a proud nickname for the Dominican Republic.

The Taíno were not a primitive people. They were skilled farmers who cultivated cassava, sweet potato, maize, and tobacco. They built bohíos — round thatched houses — in organised villages led by chiefs called caciques. They had a complex spiritual life, a tradition of oral storytelling, and a sophisticated understanding of the land and sea around them.

What Happened to the Taíno

The story of what followed Columbus's arrival is one of the most devastating in human history. Within fifty years of contact, the Taíno population of Hispaniola — estimated at several hundred thousand before 1492 — had collapsed almost entirely. Disease, forced labour in the gold mines, violence, and the destruction of their food systems combined to produce a catastrophe that left almost no survivors by the mid-1500s.

For a long time, historians described the Taíno as "extinct." That framing has been challenged in recent decades. Genetic studies and community research have shown that Taíno ancestry persists in the Caribbean population, including in the Dominican Republic. The story is more complicated — and more hopeful — than simple extinction.

"When I walk through the countryside here, I see cassava growing the same way it has grown for a thousand years. The Taíno taught this island how to feed itself. That knowledge didn't disappear — it became part of the place." — Dre

The Taíno Words You Already Know

One of the most striking legacies of the Taíno is linguistic. Dozens of words in everyday Spanish — and through Spanish, in English — come directly from the Taíno language. You have been using them without knowing it.

📖 Words from the Taíno language:
  • Canoe — from canoa, the Taíno word for their dugout boats
  • Hurricane — from huracán, the Taíno storm spirit
  • Hammock — from hamaca, the woven sleeping net they invented
  • Barbecue — from barbacoa, their wooden frame for cooking over fire
  • Tobacco — from tabaco, the rolled leaf they smoked in ceremony
  • Cassava — from casabe, their staple crop, still eaten across the Caribbean today

Where to Find Taíno History in the DR

The most significant Taíno archaeological site in the Dominican Republic is the Parque Nacional Los Haitises, on the northeastern coast. The park contains caves with Taíno petroglyphs and pictographs — images carved and painted into rock by people who lived here long before European contact. The cave art includes faces, animals, and ceremonial symbols. It is genuinely moving to stand in front of it.

The Museo del Hombre Dominicano in Santo Domingo has one of the best collections of Taíno artefacts in the Caribbean — cemíes (spiritual figures), pottery, tools, and jewellery. If you spend a day in Santo Domingo, this museum is worth two hours of your time.

💡 Dre's Tip: If you visit Los Haitises, go with a local guide who knows the caves. The park is large and the cave sites are not always well-marked. A good guide will also explain the context — what the images mean, how the Taíno used these spaces — which makes the difference between seeing rock art and actually understanding it.

The Taíno in Daily Life Today

Beyond the museums and archaeological sites, Taíno influence is woven into ordinary Dominican life in ways most people don't notice. Casabe — the flatbread made from cassava — is still sold at markets and eaten at family tables. The word bohío still describes a simple rural house. Place names across the country come from Taíno: Higüey, Azua, Baní, Cotuí.

The Taíno relationship with the land — their understanding of which plants heal, which soils grow which crops, how to read the weather from the sea — passed into Dominican agricultural knowledge and has never entirely left. When you eat a plate of yuca or drink a juice made from chinola, you are eating food that the Taíno cultivated on this island for centuries before anyone else arrived.

That continuity is worth knowing about. It makes the island more interesting, and it gives the history here a depth that goes far beyond the colonial story most visitors encounter.

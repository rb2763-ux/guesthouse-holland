Eco-Tourism in the Dominican Republic

By André de Broeders • ECO-Tourguide #001 • March 17, 2026

When I arrived in the Dominican Republic over 30 years ago, the concept of eco-tourism was virtually unknown here. Today, as the country's first certified ECO-Tourguide (certificate #001), I've witnessed the transformation of how we approach sustainable travel in this Caribbean paradise.

What Makes Eco-Tourism Different?

Eco-tourism isn't just about visiting nature—it's about protecting it while you're there. After three decades of guiding visitors through the Dominican Republic's most pristine locations, I've learned that the best travel experiences are those that leave no trace except memories.

The Dominican Republic offers incredible biodiversity: from the highest peak in the Caribbean (Pico Duarte) to the lowest point (Lago Enriquillo), from tropical rainforests to desert landscapes. But this natural wealth requires careful stewardship.

Top Eco-Tourism Destinations

Los Haitises National Park

This protected area on the northeastern coast is a maze of limestone karst formations, mangrove forests, and caves with ancient Taíno petroglyphs. When I first started bringing visitors here in the 1990s, there were no regulations. Today, certified guides are mandatory—and for good reason. The park's fragile ecosystem needs protection from well-meaning but uninformed tourists.

Eco-Tip: Visit Los Haitises with a certified guide who understands the importance of staying on marked trails and respecting wildlife viewing distances. The park's bird colonies are particularly sensitive to disturbance.

Jarabacoa and the Central Mountains

Known as the "Dominican Alps," this region offers sustainable adventure tourism: hiking, canyoning, and river rafting that respects the mountain environment. Local communities have embraced eco-tourism as an alternative to deforestation, creating a model that benefits both people and nature.

Samaná Peninsula

Every winter, humpback whales migrate to Samaná Bay to breed and give birth. Whale watching here is strictly regulated—boats must maintain distance, engines must be turned off near whales, and swimming with them is prohibited. These rules ensure that tourism doesn't disrupt these magnificent creatures.

Sustainable Practices for Travelers

After 30 years of guiding, here's what I tell every visitor about traveling responsibly in the Dominican Republic:

  • Choose local guides: Certified local guides not only provide better insights but also ensure tourism revenue stays in the community.
  • Respect wildlife: Never feed wild animals, touch coral reefs, or remove shells or plants from natural areas.
  • Minimize plastic use: Bring a reusable water bottle. The Dominican Republic struggles with plastic waste, and every bottle counts.
  • Support local businesses: Eat at local restaurants, buy from local artisans, stay in locally-owned accommodations.
  • Learn basic Spanish: Even a few phrases show respect for local culture and enhance your experience.

The Evolution of Eco-Tourism

When I received my ECO-Tourguide certification in the early 1990s, I was literally the first. The concept was so new that many people didn't understand why it mattered. "The island is big," they'd say. "How can a few tourists hurt anything?"

But I'd seen what happened in other Caribbean destinations where uncontrolled tourism degraded the very attractions that brought visitors in the first place. I was determined that the Dominican Republic would learn from those mistakes.

Today, eco-tourism is recognized as essential to the country's tourism strategy. Protected areas have expanded, regulations have been strengthened, and a new generation of guides is being trained in sustainable practices. It's been a long journey, but the results speak for themselves: the Dominican Republic's natural treasures remain largely intact.

Community-Based Eco-Tourism

Some of the most rewarding eco-tourism experiences happen in small communities that have organized to share their environment with visitors. In places like Río Limpio or Mata Grande, local families offer homestays, guide hikes, and demonstrate traditional sustainable farming methods.

These experiences provide authentic cultural exchange while generating income that helps communities protect their natural resources. It's a win-win that represents the future of Dominican tourism.

Looking Forward

At 80 years old, I'm still guiding tours and still passionate about protecting this island I've called home for over three decades. Climate change, overdevelopment, and pollution remain threats, but I'm optimistic. The Dominican people increasingly understand that their natural heritage is their greatest asset.

For visitors, the message is simple: come, enjoy, but leave only footprints. The Dominican Republic's natural beauty has survived for millennia. With responsible tourism, it will survive for millennia more.

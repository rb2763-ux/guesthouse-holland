Family Travel in the Dominican Republic: A Guide for All Ages

By André de Broeders • ECO-Tourguide #001 • March 31, 2026

I am eighty years old. I have watched three generations of families arrive in the Dominican Republic — nervous parents with toddlers in tow, grandparents who thought the tropics were not for them, teenagers who arrived sulking and left transformed. After thirty years as ECO-Tourguide #001, I can say this with confidence: the Dominican Republic is one of the finest family destinations on earth.

But only if you do it right. The resort version of family travel here is fine. The real version is extraordinary. Let me explain the difference.

Why the DR Works for Families

The Dominican Republic has something that many popular family destinations have lost: genuine warmth toward children. Dominicans adore children. Not in the polite, professional way of a hotel that has a kids' club — in the real way, where a stranger at a colmado will offer your child a mango and teach them three words of Spanish before you've finished your drink.

Children are not a nuisance here. They are welcomed into restaurants, invited into conversations, and treated as full participants in social life. For families traveling with young children, this changes everything. You are not managing a child in a foreign country. You are sharing a country that has already decided your child belongs.

For older children and teenagers, the DR offers something equally valuable: reality. Not the sanitized, curated reality of a theme park, but actual life in a developing country — the beauty, the complexity, the resilience of people who have built something remarkable with limited resources. I have watched teenagers arrive glued to their phones and leave asking serious questions about the world. That is worth more than any beach.

The Best Experiences for Families

The Ocean: Start Here

The Caribbean Sea on the south coast — where Juan Dolio sits — is calm, warm, and extraordinarily clear. The waves are gentle enough for small children. The water temperature hovers around 28 degrees year-round. There are no dangerous currents at the main beaches.

For families with children who have never snorkeled, the reef just offshore from Juan Dolio is a perfect introduction. The fish are abundant, the visibility is excellent, and the depth is manageable. I have taken children as young as six on their first snorkel here. The look on their faces when they see a parrotfish for the first time is something I never tire of.

Practical note: Bring your own snorkel gear if possible — rental equipment varies in quality. Children's masks are particularly hard to find in the right sizes locally. A good-fitting mask makes the difference between a magical experience and a frustrating one.

The Countryside: Where the Real DR Lives

The interior of the Dominican Republic is almost entirely unknown to tourists, and it is where I take families who want something beyond the beach. The Cordillera Central — the mountain range that runs through the heart of the island — is a different world from the coast.

Jarabacoa, in the mountains, offers white-water rafting, waterfall hikes, and cool temperatures that feel miraculous after the coastal heat. Families with children over ten can handle the easier rafting routes. The waterfalls — Salto de Jimenoa, Salto Baiguate — are accessible by short hikes and reward you with natural pools for swimming.

The coffee and cacao farms in the Cibao valley offer tours that children find genuinely fascinating. Watching chocolate go from a pod on a tree to something recognizable is a lesson that sticks. The farmers who run these tours are proud of their work and excellent with curious children.

Whale Watching in Samaná: A Once-in-a-Lifetime Experience

Every year from January to March, humpback whales migrate to Samaná Bay to breed and give birth. It is one of the largest humpback gatherings in the world, and it happens just a few hours from Juan Dolio.

I have taken families to Samaná many times. I have never had a child who was not completely overwhelmed by the experience. A forty-ton whale breaching twenty meters from a small boat — there is no screen, no simulation, no theme park ride that comes close to that. It is real, it is wild, and it is available to anyone willing to make the drive.

Timing matters: The whale season runs January through March. Book boats in advance — the reputable operators fill up quickly. Avoid the cheapest options; a good captain knows where the whales are and keeps a respectful distance. The whales are not a show. They are wild animals doing something ancient and important.

Santo Domingo: History That Children Can Touch

The Zona Colonial of Santo Domingo is the oldest European city in the Americas. The streets are cobblestone, the buildings are five hundred years old, and the history is layered and complex. For families with older children, it is an extraordinary classroom.

The Alcázar de Colón — the palace built by Diego Columbus, son of Christopher Columbus — is well-preserved and fascinating. The Catedral Primada de América, the oldest cathedral in the New World, is still in use. Walking these streets with a knowledgeable guide, children begin to understand that history is not something that happened somewhere else. It happened here, in these specific streets, in these specific buildings.

The Malecón seafront is perfect for evenings — wide, safe, full of families doing exactly what you are doing. Ice cream vendors, musicians, children on bicycles. It is the Dominican Republic at its most relaxed and welcoming.

Practical Matters for Families

Health and Safety

The Dominican Republic is safe for families who exercise normal caution. The tourist areas are well-patrolled. The main risks are the same as anywhere: petty theft, traffic, and the sun.

The sun here is serious. Children burn faster than adults realize, and the Caribbean sun at midday is genuinely dangerous. Hats, high-SPF sunscreen applied frequently, and shade between noon and three PM are not optional. I have seen families ruin their entire trip because they underestimated the first day's sun.

Tap water is not safe to drink. Bottled water is cheap and available everywhere. This is not a hardship — it is simply a habit to establish on day one.

  • Mosquito repellent: Essential, especially at dawn and dusk. DEET-based repellents work best.
  • Travel insurance: Always. Medical facilities in tourist areas are adequate, but evacuation insurance is worth having.
  • Cash: Many local restaurants and markets are cash-only. Keep small bills available.
  • Shoes for the beach: Reef shoes protect against sea urchins, which are common on rocky sections of coastline.

Food: Better Than You Expect

Dominican food is genuinely child-friendly. Rice, beans, chicken, plantains — these are familiar flavors that even picky eaters tend to accept. The national dish, La Bandera (rice, beans, and meat), is served everywhere and costs almost nothing at local restaurants.

Fresh fruit is extraordinary here. Mangoes, papayas, pineapples, passion fruit — all available at roadside stands for a few pesos. Children who have only ever eaten supermarket fruit are often astonished by what fruit actually tastes like when it ripens on the tree.

For families worried about food safety, stick to cooked food and peeled fruit. Avoid raw salads at smaller establishments. The larger restaurants in tourist areas maintain good standards.

A Word About Resorts

I understand why families choose all-inclusive resorts. The convenience is real. The predictability is comforting when you are traveling with children. I do not dismiss it.

But I have watched too many families spend a week in the Dominican Republic and leave knowing nothing about it. They saw a beach, a pool, a buffet, and a choreographed merengue show. They did not see the country.

My suggestion: use a resort as a base if you need the security, but leave it. Take one day trip to the mountains. Spend one evening at a local colmado. Visit one market, one farm, one waterfall that is not on the resort's excursion list. Your children will remember those days long after they have forgotten the pool.

Guest House Holland advantage: Staying in Juan Dolio rather than a large resort puts you in a real community. Your children will interact with Dominican children. You will shop at local markets. You will eat where locals eat. This is not roughing it — it is simply living, briefly, as people here actually live.

What Families Take Home

After thirty years of guiding families through this country, I have noticed something consistent. The families who engage — who leave the resort, who talk to people, who let their children get a little dirty and a little lost — those families come back. Sometimes with the same children, now grown, bringing their own children.

The Dominican Republic has a way of getting into people. The warmth is real. The beauty is real. The complexity and the resilience of the people are real. Children sense this. They may not have the words for it at eight years old, but they carry it.

I have been carrying it for thirty years. I expect to carry it for whatever years remain.

Come with your family. Let the country do what it does.

— André (Dre) de Broeders, ECO-Tourguide #001, Juan Dolio, Dominican Republic

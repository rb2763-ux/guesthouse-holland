Rum, Mama Juana, and What to Drink in the DR: Dre's Honest Guide

By Dre · June 5, 2026 · 6 min read

Dre — 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001

I've been drinking Dominican rum for more than thirty years. I know what's worth your money and what isn't. Here's the honest version.

Most visitors to the Dominican Republic drink whatever is put in front of them at the all-inclusive bar and go home thinking they've experienced Dominican rum. They haven't. The rum served at resort swim-up bars is usually the cheapest available, mixed with fruit juice and ice until the rum itself is barely detectable. That's fine — it's cold and it's refreshing — but it has nothing to do with what Dominican rum actually is.

The DR produces some of the finest aged rum in the world. The sugar cane has been grown here since the sixteenth century, the distilling tradition is centuries old, and the best Dominican rums can stand alongside the finest spirits from anywhere. Here is what you need to know.

The Rums Worth Knowing

Brugal is the most widely available Dominican rum and the one you'll encounter most often. The standard Brugal Añejo — the amber bottle — is a decent everyday rum, dry and clean, good with cola or on ice. But the rum worth seeking out is Brugal 1888, a double-aged expression finished in sherry casks. It's smooth, complex, and genuinely excellent. You can find it in most supermarkets and liquor stores for a fraction of what you'd pay for a comparable Scotch or cognac.

Barceló is the other major Dominican brand, and its premium expression — Barceló Imperial — is one of the great value rums of the Caribbean. Rich, slightly sweet, with notes of vanilla and dried fruit. Drink it neat or with a single large ice cube. Don't mix it with anything.

"My rule with good rum is simple: if it costs more than 800 pesos a bottle, drink it straight. If it costs less, mix it however you like. The rum will tell you what it wants." — Dre

Oliver & Oliver is a smaller Dominican producer making some of the most interesting rum on the island — their Opthimus range, aged in cognac and port casks, is exceptional and largely unknown outside the DR. If you see it, buy a bottle. You won't find it easily at home.

💡 Dre's Tip: The best place to buy rum is not the airport duty-free — the selection is limited and the prices are not as good as they appear. Go to a supermercado (supermarket) or a dedicated liquor store. In Juan Dolio, the Nacional supermarket on the main road carries a good selection. You'll pay local prices and have far more choice.

Mama Juana: The DR's Most Misunderstood Drink

Every visitor to the Dominican Republic will be offered Mama Juana at some point, usually by someone who describes it as an aphrodisiac and a cure for everything from back pain to poor circulation. The claims are exaggerated. The drink itself is genuinely interesting.

Mama Juana is made by steeping a mixture of tree bark, herbs, and roots in rum, red wine, and honey. The recipe varies from family to family — every Dominican household that makes it has its own version, passed down through generations. The result is a dark, slightly bitter, aromatic liqueur that tastes unlike anything else.

The best Mama Juana is homemade. The versions sold in tourist shops — pre-bottled, with the bark and roots visible through the glass — are usually mediocre. If you're offered a glass by a local family, accept it. That's the real thing.

💡 Dre's Tip: Mama Juana is traditionally drunk as a shot, not sipped. It's strong — usually around 30% alcohol — and the flavour is intense. Don't try to drink it like a cocktail. One small glass is enough to understand what it is. Two is enough to understand why people like it.

Beyond Rum: What Else to Drink

Presidente is the national beer, brewed in Santiago, and it is the right drink for the climate. Cold, light, and refreshing — it goes with everything and costs almost nothing. There is also Presidente Light for those who want less alcohol, and Bohemia, a slightly more premium Dominican lager that is worth trying.

For non-alcoholic options, the fresh fruit juices available at local juguerías (juice bars) are extraordinary. Chinola (passion fruit), tamarindo, lechosa (papaya), and guanábana (soursop) are all worth trying. Order them without sugar if you can — the fruit is sweet enough on its own, and the unsweetened versions are far more interesting.

Morir soñando — literally "to die dreaming" — is the classic Dominican drink: orange juice blended with evaporated milk and sugar over ice. It sounds strange and tastes wonderful. Every Dominican grew up drinking it. Try one.

Slow Travel in the Dominican Republic: Why Staying Longer Changes Everything

By Dre Broeders • 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001 • April 7, 2026

I arrived in the Dominican Republic more than thirty years ago and never left. That was not the plan. I came for a few months, the way most people do — with a return ticket, a schedule, and a list of things to see. Then something happened that I did not expect: the country slowed me down. And I realized, for the first time in my life, that slow was better.

Most tourists arrive here with seven days and a mission. They want the beach, the mountains, the colonial city, the whale watching, the waterfalls — all of it, compressed into a week. They leave exhausted, having seen everything and experienced almost nothing. I have watched this pattern repeat itself for three decades. It is the most common mistake visitors make.

The Dominican Republic does not reveal itself to people in a hurry. It rewards patience. It rewards the traveler who stays long enough to become, briefly, a neighbor rather than a guest.

What Slow Travel Actually Means

Slow travel is not about doing less. It is about going deeper. Instead of visiting six places in seven days, you stay in one place for two weeks and actually learn it. You find the bakery that opens at five in the morning. You learn which colmado has the coldest beer. You know the name of the man who sells mangoes from a cart on Tuesday mornings, and he knows yours.

This sounds simple. It is not. Most travelers are conditioned to optimize — to extract maximum sights per day, maximum value per dollar, maximum Instagram content per hour. Slow travel requires unlearning that instinct. It requires sitting on a porch in the afternoon heat doing nothing in particular, and being genuinely comfortable with that.

The Dominican Republic is extraordinarily good at teaching this lesson. The culture here operates on a different relationship with time. Things happen when they happen. Plans are suggestions. The afternoon rain that cancels your tour is not a disaster — it is an invitation to sit under a roof and talk to whoever is sitting next to you. Some of my best conversations in thirty years have happened during unexpected rain.

The slow travel mindset: Arrive with a loose framework, not a fixed itinerary. Know what you want to feel, not just what you want to see. Leave room for the unplanned — the neighbor who invites you to a birthday party, the fisherman who offers to take you out at dawn, the road that leads somewhere better than where you were going.

Juan Dolio: A Base Worth Knowing

Juan Dolio, where Guest House Holland sits, is not the most famous destination in the Dominican Republic. That is precisely why it works so well for slow travel. It is a real community — not a resort enclave, not a tourist strip — where Dominican families live alongside the small number of foreigners who have discovered that this is a better way to experience the country.

The beach here is calm and largely uncrowded on weekdays. The reef offshore is healthy and accessible. The town has everything you need — markets, restaurants, pharmacies, hardware stores — without the manufactured atmosphere of the big resort zones. Life happens here at its own pace, and that pace is contagious.

I have watched guests arrive at Guest House Holland wound tight from their flights and their jobs and their lives, and I have watched them unwind over two weeks in a way that a week in a resort never achieves. By day ten, they are sleeping without alarms. By day twelve, they have stopped checking their phones every twenty minutes. By the time they leave, they look like different people.

The Rhythm of a Slow Week

A slow week in Juan Dolio looks nothing like a resort week. You wake when you wake. You walk to the beach before the sun gets serious. You have breakfast at the place run by the woman who makes the best mangú on the coast — you know this because you went back three times and asked her how she does it, and she told you, and now you are friends.

In the afternoon, when the heat peaks, you rest. This is not laziness. This is intelligence. The Dominican Republic has been teaching this lesson for five hundred years. The people who built this country understood that fighting the afternoon sun is a losing battle. Rest, then emerge in the late afternoon when the light turns golden and the temperature drops and the whole coast comes alive.

Evenings are for the colmado, for conversation, for the kind of unhurried dinner that takes three hours not because the service is slow but because nobody wants to leave.

What You Notice When You Stop Rushing

Slow travel reveals things that fast travel cannot. When you walk the same road every morning for two weeks, you begin to see it. The tree that flowers on day nine. The dog that has claimed the corner by the pharmacy as his territory and enforces it with great dignity. The way the light changes on the water between six and seven in the morning — a show that repeats every day and is never exactly the same.

You also begin to understand the people. Dominican culture is warm but not immediately open. Trust is built over time, over repeated encounters, over the accumulation of small recognitions. The vendor who ignored you on day one will greet you by name on day ten. The neighbor who nodded politely will invite you for coffee on day twelve. These connections are not available to the seven-day tourist. They require time.

A practical observation: After two weeks in one place, you will spend less money than you would in two weeks moving between destinations. You stop paying tourist prices because you are no longer obviously a tourist. You know where locals eat, where locals shop, which taxi driver is honest and which is not. Local knowledge has real economic value.

Day Trips, Not Destination Hopping

Slow travel does not mean staying in one spot for two weeks without moving. It means having a base and making excursions — returning each evening to somewhere that feels, briefly, like home.

From Juan Dolio, the entire country is accessible as a day trip. Santo Domingo is thirty minutes west — close enough to visit the Zona Colonial in the morning and be back for sunset. The mountains of Jarabacoa are two hours north — a long day, but manageable. The Samaná Peninsula, with its waterfalls and whale-watching, is three hours east.

The difference between day-tripping and destination-hopping is enormous. When you return to the same place each evening, you carry the day's experiences back to a context that is becoming familiar. You process them differently. You sleep better. You wake up the next morning with a clearer sense of where you are and why you came.

The Art of Doing Nothing

I want to say something that travel writers rarely say: some of the best days I have had in thirty years in the Dominican Republic were days when I did nothing in particular. I sat on the beach. I watched the pelicans. I read a book. I talked to whoever sat down nearby. I ate when I was hungry and slept when I was tired.

These days feel wasteful when you are planning them. They feel essential when you are living them. The Dominican Republic has a quality that I can only describe as presence — a way of making the immediate moment feel sufficient. You do not need to be somewhere else. You do not need to be doing something more impressive. You are here, and here is enough.

This is the thing that slow travelers bring home that fast travelers do not: not photographs of more places, but a genuine experience of rest. Real rest, the kind that changes something in you rather than just pausing it.

Practical Advice for Slow Travelers

  • Book at least two weeks: One week is not enough to slow down. The first three days are decompression. The real experience begins around day four.
  • Choose accommodation with a kitchen: Cooking some of your own meals — with ingredients from local markets — connects you to the place in a way that restaurants alone cannot.
  • Learn ten words of Spanish: You do not need fluency. You need enough to greet people properly, to say thank you in a way that lands, to make the basic gestures of respect that open doors.
  • Leave your itinerary loose: Plan one or two anchor activities per week. Leave the rest open. The best things that happen to slow travelers are things they did not plan.
  • Walk more than you drive: The Dominican Republic reveals itself at walking pace. Things you would miss from a car — the smell of a bakery, a conversation happening on a stoop, a view through a gate — become part of your experience.
On connectivity: Consider reducing your phone use deliberately. Not eliminating it — that is neither practical nor necessary — but reducing it. The Dominican Republic is a country that rewards attention. The more attention you give it, the more it gives back.

Why I Never Left

People ask me sometimes why I stayed. I came from the Netherlands, a country of order and efficiency and excellent infrastructure. The Dominican Republic is none of those things, at least not in the same way. The roads are unpredictable. The bureaucracy is creative. The electricity has opinions of its own.

But I stayed because this country taught me something I could not have learned anywhere else: that a life lived at the pace of the place you are in is richer than a life lived at the pace of your ambitions. I have accomplished less here, by certain measures, than I might have accomplished elsewhere. I have lived more.

That is what slow travel offers. Not a vacation from your life, but a brief experience of a different way of living — one that might, if you let it, change how you live when you return.

Come for two weeks. Stay in one place. Let the country find you.

— Dre Broeders, 🌿 ECO-Tourguide #001, Juan Dolio, Dominican Republic
